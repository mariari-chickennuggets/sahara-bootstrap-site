Travelling Sahara

A journey into the Sahara can be as varied as the desert itself, but there are some quintessential experiences. Most begin in the gateway towns where expeditions out into the desert are organised. Often oases, these towns are centres of Saharan culture, places where the architecture emerges naturally from the earth and the slow pace of life has changed little in centuries. For exploring beyond town or village limits, there are two major means of getting around. A slow march across the sands astride a camel re-enacts the great camel caravans of Saharan lore â€“ on a camel safari, travellers slow down to a pace well suited to the Saharaâ€™s unforgiving climate, allowing you to appreciate details and pass through this spectacular terrain at one with your surroundings. In a 4WD expedition, travellers can range further, stirring up the sand as you tick off a list of iconic Saharan landscapes. Sleeping between four walls is an experience that ends in the towns. Out amid the sands or remote Saharan mountains, evenings are spent around a campfire, and a soft bed of sand is the night-time mattress of choice. Most Saharan excursions carry tents, but many travellers prefer to sleep outdoors beneath the clearest show of stars on earth.

The Tuareg people, known as the 'Blue Men of the Sahara', have lived in the desert for centuries. Their stories of survival, trade, and navigating the vast sands have been passed down through generations.

Essential Sahara Travel Tips

Your guide to surviving and thriving in the desert.

Packing Essentials

  • Sun Protection: Wide-brimmed hat, sunscreen (SPF 50+), sunglasses.
  • Light, Loose Clothing: Breathable fabrics like cotton to stay cool.
  • Water Supply: Carry at least 2 liters of water per person, per day.
  • Desert Footwear: Comfortable, closed shoes to protect from hot sand.

Pro Travel Tips

  • Use a Local Guide: They know the safest routes and hidden gems.
  • Hydration is Key: Drink regularly, even if you donâ€™t feel thirsty.
  • Stargazing: The Sahara offers incredible night skies. Bring a telescope if possible.
  • Stay Alert: Watch for weather changes, particularly sandstorms.

Safety & Health Tips

  • Avoid Overexposure: Take regular breaks in the shade to avoid heatstroke.
  • First-Aid Kit: Always have a basic first-aid kit for minor injuries or ailments.
  • Travel Insurance: Ensure your insurance covers desert treks.
  • Time Your Travel: Avoid traveling during peak heat hours (12-4 PM).
